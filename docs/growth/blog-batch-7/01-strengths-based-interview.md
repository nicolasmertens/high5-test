## Why Strengths-Based Interview Questions Work

Traditional interview questions focus on experience and skills. "Tell me about a time you managed a project." These questions tell you what a candidate has done, but not what they do naturally — the things that come effortlessly and energize them.

Strengths-based questions flip the approach. Instead of asking about past experience, they ask about natural patterns: what comes easily, what drains energy, what feels automatic. The answers reveal how a candidate is wired, not just what they have been trained to do.

This guide gives you practical strengths-based interview questions organized by domain, along with what to listen for in the answers.

## General Strengths-Based Questions

Start with these broad questions to identify a candidate's natural patterns:

- "What do you do so well that it surprises you when others struggle with it?"
- "What kind of work feels effortless to you — things you could do all day without getting tired?"
- "What tasks do you procrastinate on, even though you know how to do them?"
- "When you are at your best, what are you doing?"
- "What do people consistently come to you for help with?"
- "What did you love doing as a child that you still do in some form today?"

What to listen for: Genuine enthusiasm, quick recall, specific examples. When people describe their strengths, they light up. When they describe non-strengths, they hesitate or use vague language.

## Questions by Strength Domain

### Executing Domain

These strengths are about getting things done — delivering results, organizing work, and following through.

Questions:
- "Tell me about a project you took from idea to completion. What was your specific role in making it happen?"
- "How do you handle a situation where a project is falling behind schedule?"
- "Describe a time you had to be extremely organized to meet a goal."
- "What does your typical workday look like when you are being productive?"

What to listen for: Specific actions taken, emphasis on completion, comfort with structure and routine, ability to break large goals into steps.

### Influencing Domain

These strengths are about impact — taking charge, persuading others, and driving outcomes.

Questions:
- "Describe a time you convinced someone to change their mind. How did you approach it?"
- "When you walk into a room, what role do you naturally take?"
- "Tell me about a time you took initiative when no one asked you to."
- "How do you handle situations where you need to influence people who do not report to you?"

What to listen for: Confidence in taking action, comfort with conflict, ability to read a room and adjust approach, stories about mobilizing others.

### Relationship Building Domain

These strengths are about connection — building trust, understanding others, and creating team cohesion.

Questions:
- "How do you build trust with someone you have just met?"
- "Tell me about a time you helped two people or groups find common ground."
- "What do you do when you sense a colleague is struggling but has not said anything?"
- "How do you handle working with someone whose communication style is very different from yours?"

What to listen for: Empathy, patience, ability to describe others' perspectives, comfort with emotional situations, investment in long-term relationships.

### Strategic Thinking Domain

These strengths are about insight — analyzing information, seeing patterns, and generating ideas.

Questions:
- "How do you approach a problem you have never encountered before?"
- "Tell me about a time you spotted a pattern or connection that others missed."
- "When you need to learn something new quickly, what is your process?"
- "Describe a complex topic you have recently learned about. How would you explain it to someone unfamiliar?"

What to listen for: Curiosity, comfort with ambiguity, ability to synthesize information, pattern recognition, thoughtful questions about the role and company.

## Red Flags in Strengths-Based Answers

- "I am good at everything" — suggests lack of self-awareness, not breadth of talent
- Vague or generic answers without specific examples — may indicate the candidate has not reflected on their strengths
- Only listing technical skills — strengths are about natural patterns, not trained abilities
- Answers that sound rehearsed from standard interview preparation — follow up with "why" questions to go deeper

## Strengths-Based Hiring Is Not About Filtering Types

Strengths-based interviews help you understand how a candidate is wired. They do not tell you whether someone is qualified. Use them alongside skills assessments, experience evaluation, and reference checks.

The goal is not to hire people with specific strengths. It is to understand a candidate's natural patterns so you can:
- Place them in roles that use their strengths
- Pair them with colleagues who have complementary strengths
- Manage them in ways that align with how they work best

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are strengths-based interview questions legal?

Yes. Strengths-based questions focus on natural patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving — not on protected characteristics. They are widely used by major employers including Gallup, Google, and Deloitte. Avoid any questions that could be interpreted as asking about mental health conditions or disabilities.

### How do strengths-based questions differ from behavioral questions?

Behavioral questions ask "tell me about a time when..." and focus on past experience. Strengths-based questions ask "what do you do naturally?" and focus on inherent patterns. Both are valuable — use them together. Behavioral questions tell you what happened; strengths questions tell you why.

### Should I have candidates take a strengths test before the interview?

You can, but it is not required. Having a candidate take a <a href="/free-strengths-test">free Strengths assessment</a> before the interview gives both of you a framework for discussion. However, strengths-based questions work even without a formal test.

### Can strengths-based interviews reduce hiring bias?

They can help, when used correctly. By focusing on natural patterns rather than credentials or pedigree, strengths-based questions can surface talent that traditional interviews miss. However, like any interview method, they can also introduce bias if the interviewer unconsciously favors strengths similar to their own.

### How many strengths-based questions should I ask in an interview?

Three to five questions in a 45-minute interview is a good balance. Start with one or two general questions, then follow up based on the candidate's answers. Do not try to cover all four domains in one interview — depth in fewer areas produces better insights.
