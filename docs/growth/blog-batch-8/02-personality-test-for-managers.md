## Why Managers Need Personality Tests

Most management training teaches skills — delegation, feedback, planning. These are necessary but insufficient. The harder part of management is self-awareness: understanding your defaults, blind spots, and how your style affects each person on your team differently.

Personality tests give managers a structured way to understand themselves and adapt to others. Not by becoming someone else, but by knowing when to lean into their natural style and when to adjust.

This guide covers how four personality frameworks apply specifically to management.

## DISC: Your Management Communication Style

DISC tells you how you communicate expectations, give feedback, and handle conflict — the three most important management behaviors.

### If You Are High D

You manage by setting clear goals and expecting results. You are direct, fast-paced, and comfortable with difficult conversations.

Adjustments to make:
- Slow down for S-style employees who need context before direction
- Bring data for C-style employees who need evidence, not just authority
- Ask questions before giving answers — your instinct is to tell, but sometimes your team needs to be asked

### If You Are High I

You manage through relationships, enthusiasm, and positive energy. You make work enjoyable and people feel valued around you.

Adjustments to make:
- Create structure and follow-through — your enthusiasm can outpace execution
- Have the difficult conversations you tend to avoid
- Write down decisions and share them — your verbal agreements may not stick

### If You Are High S

You manage through patience, consistency, and genuine care for your team. People trust you and feel safe working with you.

Adjustments to make:
- Make unpopular decisions faster — consensus is valuable but not always possible
- Advocate for your team upward — do not wait to be asked
- Address underperformance early — patience becomes enablement when stretched too far

### If You Are High C

You manage through clarity, systems, and quality standards. Your team always knows what "good" looks like.

Adjustments to make:
- Give positive feedback that is not tied to a deliverable — your team needs to feel valued as people, not just producers of quality work
- Speed over perfection for time-sensitive decisions
- Communicate the "why" behind your standards, not just the standards themselves

## Enneagram: Your Management Motivation

The Enneagram reveals why you manage the way you do — and what happens to your management under stress.

### Management Motivations by Type

- **Type 1:** You manage to improve things. Under stress, you become critical and rigid. Your team may feel nothing is ever good enough.
- **Type 2:** You manage to support people. Under stress, you become intrusive and resentful when your help is not reciprocated.
- **Type 3:** You manage to achieve results. Under stress, you become image-focused and may sacrifice team well-being for numbers.
- **Type 6:** You manage to create safety. Under stress, you become anxious and may micromanage out of fear.
- **Type 8:** You manage to protect and drive. Under stress, you become controlling and may intimidate without realizing it.
- **Type 9:** You manage to maintain harmony. Under stress, you become passive and may fail to address problems until they escalate.

## 16 Personalities: Your Decision-Making Style

The 16 Personalities framework helps managers understand how they make decisions and process information — and where their team might need a different approach.

### Key Management Dimensions

**Thinking managers** make decisions based on logic and efficiency. They may undervalue team morale and emotional impact. Growth: before every decision, ask "how will this affect the people involved?"

**Feeling managers** make decisions based on values and people impact. They may avoid tough but necessary choices. Growth: before every decision, ask "what does the evidence say?"

**Judging managers** prefer structure, plans, and clear timelines. They may become rigid when plans change. Growth: build flexibility into every plan — expect change.

**Perceiving managers** prefer adaptability and open-ended exploration. They may struggle with deadlines and follow-through. Growth: set external deadlines and commit to them publicly.

## Strengths: Your Management Advantage

The most effective managers spend the majority of their time using their natural strengths. They build systems or partnerships for everything else.

### The Strengths-Based Management Approach

1. **Identify your top 5 strengths.** These are your management superpowers.
2. **Design your role around them.** If Strategic is your top strength, spend more time on planning and less on execution. If Empathy is your top strength, spend more time on coaching and less on analysis.
3. **Delegate your non-strengths.** If Analytical is not in your top 5, find a team member or peer who excels at data and ask them to review your numbers.
4. **Watch for overuse.** Under stress, your strengths can become liabilities. A manager with strong Command may become bossy. A manager with strong Empathy may avoid holding people accountable.

## A Manager's Personality Development Plan

### Month 1: Self-Discovery
Take all four assessments. Write down one insight from each. Share results with your team and ask: "Does this match how you experience me?"

### Month 2: One Adjustment
Pick the single adjustment that would have the biggest impact on your team. Practice it daily for 30 days. Tell your team what you are working on — they will help hold you accountable.

### Month 3: Feedback Loop
Ask each team member: "What has changed? What still needs to change?" Write down their answers. Adjust your plan based on what you hear.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to be a certain personality type to be a good manager?

No. Effective managers come from every personality type. The common factor is self-awareness and adaptability — knowing your defaults and adjusting to the people you lead.

### Which personality test should managers take first?

DISC is the best starting point for managers because it directly describes communication and behavioral style — the most visible parts of management. Pair it with Enneagram for deeper motivation insights.

### How do I manage someone whose personality is very different from mine?

First, understand their type. Then adjust your communication to match their style, not yours. This is not about changing who you are — it is about adding tools to your management toolkit. A high-D manager can learn to slow down. A high-S manager can learn to be direct.

### Can personality tests help with performance management?

Indirectly. Understanding an employee's personality helps you give feedback in the way they receive it best. A high-D employee wants direct, brief feedback. A high-I employee needs to feel valued even when receiving criticism. A high-C employee needs specific, evidence-based examples. The content of the feedback is the same — the delivery adapts to the receiver.

### Should I share my personality results with my team?

Yes. Sharing your results builds trust and models vulnerability. Tell your team: "Here is my style. Here is what I am working on. Here is how you can help me be a better manager for you."
