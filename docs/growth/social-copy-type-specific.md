### LinkedIn — INFP vs INFJ (comparison page driver)
One of the most common mistyping pairs: INFP and INFJ.

Both are introverted, values-driven, and deeply empathetic. Both are often described as "too sensitive" for environments that mistake their depth for weakness.

But they're fundamentally different:

**INFJ** leads with introverted intuition — they form visions about the future and work systematically toward them. They're often planners.

**INFP** leads with introverted feeling — they check everything against their personal values. They're often adapters.

The easiest diagnostic: in a conflict, do you want to understand what the situation is moving toward (INFJ) or do you want to check whether the situation violates your values (INFP)?

### LinkedIn — INTJ vs INFJ (comparison page driver)
Both INTJ and INFJ are strategic, private, and rare.

They're often mistaken for each other — especially in professional settings where both show up as "quiet, visionary, hard to read."

The real difference is what drives the vision:

**INTJ** implements through logic and systems — they optimize for efficiency and excellence. Feedback is direct. Standards are non-negotiable.

**INFJ** implements through people and meaning — they optimize for human growth and impact. Feedback is careful. Relationships are essential to the vision.

Ask yourself: does your vision exist to build something excellent, or to help people become something better?

ENFPs are chronically underestimated in the hiring process.

They interview extremely well — they're warm, enthusiastic, and naturally compelling. Then six months in, they're bored and everyone wonders why performance dropped.

## ENTP Posts

### LinkedIn — ENTP at Work
The hardest thing about working with ENTPs:

They'll argue a position they don't even believe just to test whether your argument holds up.

For people who take this personally, ENTPs seem combative and exhausting. For people who understand it, ENTPs are the fastest way to stress-test any idea before it goes into the market.

If you have an ENTP on your team, don't ask them to stop challenging. Ask them to be explicit about when they're playing devil's advocate vs. when they genuinely disagree. That one norm change removes 80% of the friction.

ENTP and ENFP look almost identical from the outside.

## DISC Posts

### LinkedIn — DISC D Type
High D (Dominance) personalities get a reputation problem at work.

They're described as "too aggressive," "doesn't listen," "steamrolls the team."

Here's what's actually happening: the D style processes fast, decides fast, and moves fast — because in their worldview, slow deliberation costs more than a correctable mistake.

The issue isn't the pace. It's that most teams haven't agreed on when to defer to the D's decisiveness vs. when to slow down and gather input.

When those norms are explicit, D types become the biggest asset in the room. When they're not, they become the biggest friction point.

### LinkedIn — DISC S Type
The most underappreciated DISC style in leadership conversations: the S (Steadiness).

S types are the people who hold teams together when everything is chaotic. They absorb stress without drama. They build the trust that makes the team feel safe enough to do ambitious work. They're there consistently when others come and go.

The S style's weakness — resistance to change, preference for predictability — gets talked about constantly.

The S style's strength — being the actual backbone of organizational stability — gets mentioned far less often.

Understanding your disintegration pattern doesn't tell you to avoid stress. It tells you what to watch for so you can intervene before you're fully in it.
