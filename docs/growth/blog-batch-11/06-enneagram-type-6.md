## Who Is Enneagram Type 6?

Enneagram Type 6 — the Loyalist — is responsible, committed, and driven by a need for security, certainty, and trustworthy support. Sixes think ahead, anticipate problems, and question assumptions — including the ones everyone else takes for granted. This makes them the people who catch things others miss and who build the reliable structures that hold organizations and relationships together.

Sixes are also the Enneagram's most internally diverse type. Some Sixes express their anxiety through excessive worry and deference to authority. Others express it through reactive counter-dependency — pushing back against authority and testing everything. Both are driven by the same core dynamic: searching for what can be trusted.

## Core Motivation and Fear

**Core desire:** To have security, support, and guidance from trustworthy sources.

**Core fear:** Being without support, abandoned, or unable to survive what is coming.

This fear generates a constant scanning for threat. Sixes are anticipators. They run worst-case scenarios not because they enjoy them but because being prepared feels like safety. When they trust someone — a person, an institution, a team — they give that loyalty fully.

## 5 Strengths of Enneagram Type 6

**1. Loyalty and reliability**
When a Six is on your side, they are genuinely on your side. Their commitment to people and teams they trust is deep and durable.

**2. Anticipating problems**
Sixes spot what could go wrong before it does. This is not pessimism — it is a real skill in risk management, planning, and quality control.

**3. Building trustworthy structures**
Sixes create systems, processes, and teams that other people can depend on. They understand what makes things reliable because they think about it constantly.

**4. Courage under pressure**
Counterintuitively, Sixes can be highly courageous. When the threat is real and others are depending on them, Sixes often step up in ways that surprise even themselves.

**5. Questioning assumptions**
Sixes do not simply accept what they are told. Their skeptical questioning finds the weak points in plans, arguments, and authority — which makes those things stronger when they survive scrutiny.

## 5 Weaknesses and Growth Paths

**1. Anxiety and catastrophizing**
*Growth path:* The worst case is rarely the actual case. Practice distinguishing preparedness from dread. Your predictions are not prophecies.

**2. Testing trust in ways that undermine relationships**
*Growth path:* Some testing is legitimate. At a certain point, the test is the problem. Identify when you are inviting the betrayal you fear.

**3. Difficulty trusting your own judgment**
*Growth path:* You have good instincts. Practice acting on them before seeking external confirmation. Build a track record with yourself.

**4. Procrastination driven by fear of failure**
*Growth path:* The preparation loop can run indefinitely. Set a decision point and hold it. Imperfect action beats perfect inaction.

**5. Projecting hostility onto neutral situations**
*Growth path:* Not everyone who disagrees is a threat. Not every ambiguous situation is dangerous. Check whether the threat is real before responding to it.

## Wings

**6w5 — The Defender:** More analytical, self-reliant, and reserved. Combines the Six's focus on security with the Five's drive for independent knowledge. Often found in technical, legal, and strategic roles.

**6w7 — The Buddy:** More outgoing, fun-loving, and social. Combines security-seeking with the Seven's energy and optimism. Often warm, funny, and highly loyal within close groups.

## Famous Type 6 Examples

- **Tom Hanks** — Known for his reliability, loyalty to collaborators, and grounded warmth — a Six's earned trust made visible
- **Princess Diana** — Her questioning of the institution she married into and her fierce loyalty to people who were vulnerable are classic Six patterns
- **Mark Twain** — A skeptic who questioned authority, tradition, and received wisdom with sharp humor and genuine concern for the human cost of hypocrisy
- **J. Edgar Hoover** — A less healthy Six who built elaborate systems of surveillance as a response to pervasive anxiety and need for control

## Growth Direction

Type 6 grows toward the positive qualities of Type 9 — inner peace, trust in the present moment, and the ability to rest in security that is not contingent on external certainty. A healthy Six learns that they can trust themselves, that the world is not always hostile, and that loyalty to their own judgment is as important as loyalty to others.

The invitation: trust the ground you are standing on.
